CADAgent Use Cases

CADAgent turns plain English descriptions into real parametric geometry inside Fusion 360. Below are concrete examples of what you can build, each showing the exact prompt you would type and what CADAgent produces in your timeline. These are not screenshots or mesh files — every result is fully editable, dimensioned Fusion 360 features.

Mounting brackets

Brackets are one of the most common parts engineers need to create quickly. With CADAgent, you describe the shape, dimensions, and hole pattern in one sentence:

"Create an L-bracket, 50x30mm base, 50x40mm wall, 3mm thick, with two M4 countersunk holes on the base and two on the wall."

From this prompt, CADAgent generates a sketch for the L-profile cross section and extrudes it to form the bracket body. It then creates four separate hole features — two countersunk M4 holes positioned on the base face and two on the vertical wall face. Each hole is a native Fusion 360 hole feature, meaning the countersink angle, diameter, and depth all appear in the timeline as editable parameters. You can go back and change the M4 holes to M5, adjust the bracket thickness from 3mm to 4mm, or modify any dimension after the fact. This is the key advantage over generating an STL: the design intent is preserved.

Enclosures and housings

Electronic project enclosures usually follow a predictable workflow: sketch a rectangle, extrude it into a solid block, shell it out, and round the edges. CADAgent handles the entire sequence from a single prompt:

"Create a rectangular box 120x80x40mm with 2mm wall thickness, open top, and 5mm fillets on outer edges."

CADAgent breaks this down into four operations. First, it creates a rectangular sketch on the XY plane with 120mm and 80mm dimensions. Second, it extrudes the rectangle 40mm upward to form a solid block. Third, it applies a shell operation to hollow the body with 2mm wall thickness, removing the top face to leave an open-top box. Finally, it applies 5mm fillets to all outer vertical edges. Each operation appears as a separate feature in your Fusion 360 timeline, so you can drag the shell feature to adjust wall thickness or edit the fillet radius independently. This sketch-extrude-shell-fillet workflow is how experienced CAD users would model the part manually, which means the feature tree stays clean and predictable.

Mechanical fastener patterns

Flanges, adapter plates, and bearing mounts often require evenly spaced bolt holes on a circular pattern. Doing this manually means creating one hole, then setting up a circular pattern with the correct count and diameter. CADAgent handles both steps from a single description:

"Create a 100mm diameter flange with 6 evenly spaced M6 through-holes on a 80mm bolt circle."

CADAgent first sketches and extrudes the 100mm diameter disc. It then places a single M6 through-hole at the correct radial distance (40mm from center, on the 80mm bolt circle) and applies a circular pattern to replicate it six times at 60-degree intervals. The circular pattern is a native Fusion 360 feature, so if you later decide you need 8 holes instead of 6, you edit one parameter and the entire pattern updates. You can also change the bolt circle diameter or switch from M6 to M8 holes without rebuilding anything from scratch. This approach works for any bolt pattern — square, rectangular, or custom layouts.

Quick prototyping parts

Standoffs, spacers, bushings, and other small turned parts come up constantly during hardware prototyping. These parts are simple in geometry but tedious to model through menus. CADAgent makes them nearly instant:

"Create a cylindrical standoff, 10mm OD, 5mm ID, 15mm tall with M5 thread on the outside."

For this part, CADAgent creates two concentric circles (10mm and 5mm diameter) on the XY plane and extrudes the ring profile 15mm upward, or alternatively extrudes the outer cylinder and then uses a boolean cut to remove the inner bore. It then applies an M5 thread feature to the outer cylindrical face. Fusion 360 threads created through CADAgent are modeled threads — they carry the correct pitch, class, and designation metadata, which means they export correctly for manufacturing drawings and 3D printing. The boolean approach (extrude solid, then cut the bore) keeps the timeline clean and makes it easy to adjust the inner or outer diameter independently.

Tips for effective prompts

Getting good results from CADAgent comes down to how you phrase your prompts. Here are practical guidelines based on what works best:

  • Include specific dimensions. Always state sizes in millimeters or inches. "Create a 50x30mm plate" works much better than "create a small plate." CADAgent interprets exact numbers directly into sketch dimensions and extrusion distances.
  • Use standard engineering terminology. Terms like "countersunk," "through-hole," "fillet," "chamfer," "shell," and "thread" map directly to Fusion 360 features. The more precisely you name the operation, the more accurate the result.
  • Specify operations explicitly. If you want a hollow box, say "shell with 2mm wall thickness" rather than "make it hollow." If you want rounded edges, say "5mm fillet on all edges" rather than "round the corners." Explicit operation names reduce ambiguity.
  • Iterate on results. CADAgent works within your Fusion 360 timeline, so you can generate a base part and then refine it with follow-up prompts. Start with the overall shape, then add holes, patterns, fillets, and other details in subsequent steps. This mirrors how experienced engineers model parts — rough shape first, details second.
  • Reference planes and faces. When placing features, specify which face or plane you mean. "Two M4 holes on the top face" is clearer than "add some holes." If a part has multiple similar faces, reference them by orientation — top, bottom, front, left — to avoid misplacement.

CADAgent is designed to handle the repetitive, menu-driven work so you can focus on design decisions. The more you treat it like briefing a colleague — clear, specific, and structured — the better your results will be.
